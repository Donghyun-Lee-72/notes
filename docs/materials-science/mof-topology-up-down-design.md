# MOF Topology and Up–Down Design

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) are extended structures assembled from metal
nodes or clusters and organic linkers. Their **topology** describes the
connectivity of those components after the detailed chemistry is reduced to a
network of nodes and edges.

Topology is not the same as **ontology**. Topology asks how building units are
connected. Ontology is a formal knowledge representation that defines concepts,
properties, and relations in a domain. The 2024 *Nature Synthesis* study by Kim
and colleagues concerns MOF topology and design, not an MOF ontology.

## Why topology matters

Different chemical structures can share the same underlying net, while similar
building units can produce different nets when their connectivity, orientation,
or linker geometry changes. A topology label is therefore a compact description
of connectivity, not a complete atomic structure or synthesis recipe.

Topology helps researchers:

- compare frameworks independently of exact composition;
- reason about node connectivity and pore architecture;
- identify combinations of clusters and linkers compatible with a target net;
- classify known structures and search for unreported configurations.

## Bottom-up, top-down, and up–down design

A bottom-up design starts from available building units and asks which extended
structures they can assemble. A top-down design starts from a desired framework
or net and asks which components could realize it. Each view constrains a
different part of the design problem.

Kim and colleagues combined both directions in an **up–down approach**. For
Zr-based MOFs, they screened promising topology candidates while considering
the possible orientations of Zr₆ clusters. They reported 26 prospective
structural configurations, used ribbon representations and linker-angle
analysis to construct models, and experimentally synthesized Zr₆-based MOFs
with **bct(1)** and **scu(1)** configurations.

## What the result does and does not establish

The workflow expands the searchable chemical space by linking an abstract net
to cluster orientation and feasible linker geometry. It does not imply that
every enumerated topology is synthetically accessible. Energetics, competing
phases, precursor chemistry, solvent, modulator, temperature, kinetics, and
defects still influence whether a proposed framework forms.

The topology is also not read directly from a single diffraction spot. A
diffraction experiment supplies reciprocal-space intensities; structure
solution and refinement produce an atomic model; topological analysis then
reduces that model to its connectivity net. Disorder or intergrowth can make
this interpretation more difficult.
